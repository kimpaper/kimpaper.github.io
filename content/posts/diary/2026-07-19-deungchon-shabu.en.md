Tonight's dinner: Deungchon-style shabu-shabu, delivered — with one hack.
The beef came from the supermarket, since a tray of shabu-sliced beef costs
less than the delivery add-on and you get more of it.

![Shabu hot pot](/images/2026-07-19-shabu-1.jpg)

**What is Korean shabu-shabu?** Korea took the Japanese swish-swish hot pot
and made it its own — a spicy, deeply seasoned broth loaded with mushrooms,
minari (water dropwort), and bean sprouts. "Deungchon shabu" is a beloved
Seoul style built around that fiery broth.

![Supermarket shabu beef](/images/2026-07-19-shabu-2.jpg)

Paper-thin beef slices go into the bubbling broth for just a few seconds.

![The broth at work](/images/2026-07-19-shabu-3.jpg)

Here's the thing about Korean hot pot: the meal has **acts**. After the beef
is gone, the broth — now rich with everything that's been through it — gets
**kalguksu** (칼국수, knife-cut wheat noodles). The chewy noodles drink up
the broth.

![Fried rice, act three](/images/2026-07-19-shabu-4.jpg)

And then the finale every Korean waits for: **bokkeumbap** (볶음밥). Rice,
chopped vegetables, and an egg go into the last of the broth and get fried
down right in the pot.

![The finished fried rice](/images/2026-07-19-shabu-5.jpg)

You scrape up the crispy scorched bits at the bottom — the best part.
Broth → noodles → fried rice, the full three-act run. With this many
vegetables and mushrooms, it even feels like health food. Sort of.
